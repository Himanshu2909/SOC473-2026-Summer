# CASTE AND RACE IN INDIA: CHAPTER I - FEATURES OF THE CASTE SYSTEM

## Introduction to the Caste System

- The caste system strikes foreign visitors due to its ubiquity and its marked contrast with the social groupings prevalent in Europe or America.
    
- **Baines** notes that it takes a very short time for a casual observer to recognize the ubiquity and exoteric features of the institution.
    
- Providing a real general definition of caste is bound to fail due to the complexity of the phenomenon.
    
- **Megasthenes** (3rd Century B.C. foreigner) provided the earliest account of this institution.
    
- Megasthenes highlighted two salient features: strict prohibitions against contracting marriage outside one's caste and prohibitions against changing or mixing professions (except for the caste of philosophers).
    

## The Six Outstanding Features of Hindu Society

When ruled by the social philosophy of caste (unaffected by modern ideas of rights/duties), Hindu society possessed six distinct features.

### 1. Segmental Division of Society

- Caste-society was not a homogeneous community; it was divided into prominent groups with distinct appellations.
    
- Membership in these groups was determined strictly by **birth**, not by selection or wealth.
    
- **MacIver** contrasts Eastern and Western civilizations: in the East, status is determined by birth (rigid, creates permanent lines of cleavage), whereas in the West, it is determined by wealth (alienable, acquirable).
    
- A caste had a quasi-sovereign status; it had its own governing body called a **Panchayat** (standing or occasional council).
    
- The Panchayat regulated the conduct and morals of its members, effectively taking over matters usually dealt with by the State in its judicial capacity.
    
- **Offenses tried by Panchayats included:** forbidden social intercourse, keeping a concubine of another caste, adultery, non-payment of debt, killing a cow, insulting a Brahmin, and defying caste customs regarding feasts.
    
- **Punishments awarded by Panchayats included:** temporary or permanent out-casting, fines (often spent on common feasts or charitable purposes), feasts given to castemen, corporal punishment, and religious expiation.
    
- This quasi-sovereignty meant citizens owed moral allegiance to their caste first, creating a cultural gulf between castes.
    
- Cultural differences extended to religion (e.g., Brahmins did not worship _Grama Devata_ or village gods, which aboriginal populations bowed down to), marriage customs (Brahmins did not permit widow-marriage), and everyday life (caste fellows must carry the corpse to the cremation ground and assist in marriage feasts).
    
- British Courts in India utilized a collection of Hindu caste laws and customs made by **Steele** to ascertain diverse usages.
    

### 2. Hierarchy

- Caste society featured a definite scheme of social precedence, universally placing the **Brahmin** at the head of the hierarchy.
    
- **Exception:** In Southern India, artisan castes (like the _Kammalan_) actively disputed Brahmin supremacy and claimed equal rank.
    
- **John Fryer** (who visited India in 1670) noted this attitude of Southern artisan castes.
    
- While the extremes of the hierarchy (Brahmins at the top, degraded castes like _Holeyas_ at the bottom) were clear, the exact social precedence among intermediate castes was nebulous and heavily disputed.
    
- **Martin** observed in Bihar that people could not reliably map castes to the four traditional _Sudra_ classes because they paid little attention to the claims of lower individuals.
    

### 3. Restrictions on Feeding and Social Intercourse

Minute rules dictated what food/drink could be accepted and from whom, broadly dividing India into two belts.

**A. Northern India (Hindustan) Rules:**

- **Kachcha Food:** Food cooked with water. Only eaten if prepared by a fellow sub-caste member or a Brahmin _Guru_.
    
- **Pakka Food:** Food cooked in _ghi_ (clarified butter) without water.
    
- Northern castes were divided into five food-status groups:
    
    1. The twice-born castes.
        
    2. Castes from whom the twice-born can take "Pakka" food.
        
    3. Castes from whom the twice-born can take water (but no food).
        
    4. Castes from whom water cannot be accepted by the twice-born (but who are not untouchable).
        
    5. Untouchables (whose touch defiles the orthodox).
        
- **Kanaujia Brahmins** were highly punctilious; a proverb states that "three Kanaujias require no less than thirteen hearths."
    
- Low castes practiced untouchability among themselves (e.g., _Basor_ and _Bansphor_ would not touch a _Dom_ or _Dhobi_).
    

**B. Bengal Rules:**

- Divided into two main groups: Brahmins and Sudras.
    
- Sudras were subdivided into four classes:
    
    1. **Sat-Sudra:** (e.g., Kayasth, Nabasakh).
        
    2. **Jalacharaniya-Sudras:** Brahmins can take water from them.
        
    3. **Jalabyabaharya Sudras:** Brahmins cannot take water from them.
        
    4. **Asprisya Sudras:** Untouchables whose touch pollutes Ganges water.
        

**C. Gujarat & Southern India Rules:**

- No distinction between "Kachcha" and "Pakka" food.
    
- Castes generally refused cooked food from any caste lower than themselves, but would accept food from a Brahmin.
    
- **Distance Pollution in Malabar (Madras):** Impurity was transmitted by shadow or approaching within a certain distance.
    
    - _Shanar_ (toddy-tapper): pollutes Brahmin at **24 paces**.
        
    - _Nayar_: must not touch a Nambudiri Brahmin.
        
    - _Tiyan_: must keep **36 steps** from a Brahmin, 12 paces from a Nayar.
        
    - _Pulayan_: must keep **96 paces** from a Brahmin and cannot approach any Hindu caste.
        

### 4. Civil and Religious Disabilities and Privileges

- **Segregation:** Village structures physically segregated castes.
    
    - In Trichinopoly, separate streets existed for Brahmins, Shudras, and Panchamas (Pallans, Paraiyans, Chakkiliyans).
        
    - In Madras, agriculturists lived in the _Palli teru_.
        
    - Under Maratha/Peshwa rule, _Mahars_ and _Mangs_ were banned from entering Poona between 3 PM and 9 AM because their long shadows would defile Brahmins.
        
- **Civic Disabilities for Lower Castes:**
    
    - _Shanars_ and _Izhavas_ could not build houses above one story, carry umbrellas, wear shoes/gold, or milk cows.
        
    - In Malabar, lower caste houses were referred to as "dung-heaps" in the presence of Nambudiri Brahmins.
        
    - Women of lower castes (e.g., _Tiyan_) were required by law to leave their upper bodies bare until 1865.
        
    - In the Punjab, sweepers had to carry a broom and shout a warning of their polluting presence.
        
- **Privileges for Higher Castes (Brahmins):**
    
    - Exemptions from capital punishment, lower land assessment rates, and liberal treatment in prisons.
        
    - Only Brahmins could perform the highly sacred **Vedic** rituals, while Sudras used **Puranic** rituals.
        
    - Brahmins monopolized inner temple access and never bowed to non-Brahmins (only offering benedictions).
        
- **Factions in Madras:** Non-Brahmin castes were divided into **"right-hand"** and **"left-hand"** factions, fighting over privileges like riding horseback or raising a certain number of pillars (eleven vs. twelve) for marriage booths.
    

### 5. Lack of Choice of Occupation

- Callings were generally considered hereditary.
    
- The **priestly profession** was strictly monopolized by Brahmins.
    
- However, historical records show significant occupational flexibility in practice:
    
    - Konkanasth/Deshasth Brahmins acted as village accountants (_Kulkarnis_) and soldiers.
        
    - Kanaujia Brahmins entered the Bengal army as sepoys.
        
    - Havig Brahmins performed agricultural labor.
        
- General occupations like trading, agriculture, and military service were accessible to most castes.
    
- **Colebrooke** and **Irving** noted that occupational choice was relatively open. **Baines** clarified that a caste's traditional occupation wasn't necessarily what most of its members actively practiced.
    

### 6. Restrictions on Marriage

- **Endogamy:** Marrying strictly within one's own sub-group. Sociologist **Westermarck** called endogamy "the essence of the caste system."
    
- **Exceptions to Endogamy:**
    
    1. **Hypergamy:** Higher caste men marrying lower caste women (e.g., in Punjab hills, or younger sons of Nambudiri Brahmins consorting with Kshatriya/Nayar women in Malabar).
        
    2. Inter-caste marriage was occasionally allowed among some artisan castes in Malabar.
        
- **Sub-castes as the Real Unit:** Groups were divided into incredibly small sub-units (e.g., Banias dividing into Shrimali, Porwal, Dasa Porwal, Visa Porwal, and further by city).
    
- **Gait** argued against elevating sub-castes to the status of "castes" because it would create a bewildering multiplicity, but the text argues that sub-castes are the true unit of social organization.
    
- Higher castes were known by regional generic terms: _Ashraf_ (Bihar), _Bhadralok_ (Bengal), or _Pandhar-peshe_ (Maharashtra).
    
- **F.J. Richards** and **Russell** firmly state that the endogamous group or sub-caste is the real unit and basis of the fabric of Indian society.
    

## The Village Community: Harmony and Interdependence

Despite the rigid segregation and hierarchy, the system possessed mechanisms for civic harmony.

- **Economic and Civic Interdependence:** South Indian villages had twelve to twenty-four permanent officials/menials belonging to different castes.
    
- **Sir Charles Metcalfe** described village communities as "little republics," capable of surviving revolutions, pillage, and changing dynasties due to their internal solidarity and established usages.
    
- **Panchayat Inclusiveness:** Decisions by village councils in the Maratha country bore the signatures of almost all village servants, including untouchables like the _Mahar_ and _Mang_.
    
- **Dr. Matthai** and **Anandaranga Pillay** cited historical village meetings where Brahmins, Shudras, and Pariahs assembled together to adjudicate local disputes (e.g., punishing a Brahmin schoolmaster, resolving hereditary rights).
    
- **Ceremonial Importance of Lower Castes:** Specific rituals required the services of lower castes, temporarily elevating their status:
    
    - **Barber:** Acted as matchmaker, assistant to Brahmin priests, and matrimonial priest for lower castes (and even some _Vellalas_). The officiating priest at the Jagannath temple is a barber.
        
    - **Dom:** Handled the first logs and foundation of the funeral pyre in Benares.
        
    - **Potter (_Kumbhar_):** Presented pots to brides for propitious marriages in the Central Provinces.
        
    - **Mahar:** Kindled the Holi fire first in Berar, fixed wedding dates for other castes, and slaughtered buffalo at the Dasahra festival.
        
    - **Paraiyan:** Served as the arbitrator in boundary disputes (walking the line with a pot of water on his head) and was allowed to lord over the Brahmin during specific temple festivals.
        
- **Conclusion:** This interdependence in village life prevented the exclusivist rules of caste from completely fracturing society, creating a functional, albeit rigorously unequal, civic harmony.
