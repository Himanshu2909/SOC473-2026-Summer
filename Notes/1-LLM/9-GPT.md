### **Comprehensive Notes: Gold Governance and Goldsmithery in India**

These notes are structured to highlight critical facts, dates, terminology, and theoretical frameworks necessary for an MCQ examination, based entirely on the provided text by Sruti Kanungo and Anindita Chakrabarti.

### **1. Core Themes & Introduction**

- **Unique Status of Gold:** In India, gold has a dual demand; it is sought after for 'sacred' ritualistic purposes as well as for 'profane' economic security.
    
- **India's Market Position:** India is the second-largest consumer of gold globally, importing over 800 tonnes of gold annually.
    
- **Manufacturing Landscape:** While 70% of India's jewelry industry falls under the unorganized sector, 60-65% of the jewelry manufactured in the country is handmade.
    
- **Impact of Demonetisation (2016):** Despite expectations that the sector would be ruined, the industry recovered within a month due to the high demand during the wedding season.
    

### **2. The Gold Supply Chain**

- **Global Circulation:** The journey begins at gold mines, moves to Refinery Vaults, and then to Bullion Banks located in countries like the USA, UK, Dubai, India, and China.
    
- **National Level:** National Banks and Nominated Agencies (approved by the RBI) act as financiers and wholesalers.
    
- **Traders & Jewellers:** Gold passes down to Bullion Traders, then to Wholesale Traders, and subsequently to large, medium, and small Jewellers.
    
- **Manufacturing Level:** Jewellers provide work orders to a Master Craftsman (or middleman) who manages the Master Karigars, Journeymen, and Apprentices to create the final product.
    
- **Recycling:** Customers recycle or pawn old jewelry, returning it to the supply chain.
    

### **3. State Regulation: The Gold Control Act (GCA)**

- **Defence of India Act 1962:** This was the initial framework used to curb gold demand, heavily influenced by the 1962 Sino-Indian War.
    
- **1963 Amendments:** Under the Defence of India Rules, Part XII A was inserted, requiring dealers to declare their gold holdings within 30 days. Non-compliance was punishable by imprisonment. Morarji Desai (then Finance Minister) appealed to citizens' national conscience to stop buying gold.
    
- **Gold Control Act of 1968:** Replaced the 1963 rules, severely restricting gold manufacturing, possession, and trade.
    
    - _Restrictions:_ A certified goldsmith was legally barred from employing more than one hired laborer. Goldsmiths could not possess more than 300 grams of primary gold or receive more than 100 grams for manufacturing.
        
- **Unintended Consequences:** The GCA devastated traditional caste-based artisan families and failed to curb domestic demand. Instead, it created a massive shadow economy; smuggled gold skyrocketed from 26.27 tonnes (1955-1956) to 520 tonnes (1958-1963).
    
- **Repeal (1990):** The GCA was repealed in 1990, aligning with India's economic liberalization, which sparked a massive boom in demand and opened the market.
    

### **4. The Emergence of the "New" Goldsmiths**

- **Traditional System:** Historically, goldsmithing was a traditional caste occupation (e.g., the _Sunar_ in North India or _Soni_ in Gujarat) bound by the _jajmani_ system of reciprocal village duties.
    
- **The Shift:** Liberalization in the 1990s caused a breakdown in traditional caste/kin groups as demand outpaced the supply of traditional artisans.
    
- **The Bengali Goldsmiths:** The modern informal sector is heavily dominated by migrant workers from West Bengal, specifically from the Ghatal sub-division of West Medinipur.
    
- **Caste Profile:** A vast majority (68.33%) of these new artisans belong to the **Mahisya** caste, which is traditionally an agrarian/farming community, not a goldsmithing caste.
    
- **Catalyst for Migration:** A devastating flood in 1978 ruined crops in West Medinipur, forcing these farmers to seek alternative livelihoods in the 1980s and 1990s. They utilized their pre-existing knowledge of working with bell metal and copper to adapt to gold.
    
- **Entry Strategy (Guarantors):** Because gold is precious, new entrants absolutely require a "guarantor"—a trusted friend, relative, or neighbor from their village—to be accepted into a workshop.
    

### **5. Work Structure: Guilds, Skill, and Trust**

- **Guild-Like Hierarchy:** The informal sector operates much like a traditional guild, structured around the Master Craftsman, Journeyman, and Apprentice.
    
    - **Apprentice:** Learns basic tasks (melting, beating, squeezing machines) and is unpaid until proficient.
        
    - **Journeyman:** Paid based on the amount of gold worked on.
        
    - **Master Karigar:** Highly skilled, paid a small percentage of gold known as "loss", and often referred to by apprentices as their _guru_.
        
- **Specialized Departments:** Jewelry pieces move through highly specialized, cooperative departments such as _garti/gadhani_ (engraving), die cutting, diamond cutting, stone fitting, and _minakari_ (enamelling).
    
- **Trust and Secrecy:** Master craftsmen deliberately hide the exact quantity of gold given to junior artisans to prevent theft or unauthorized alloying.
    
- **Spatial Clustering:** To manage risk, ensure security, and facilitate the sharing of expensive machinery, workshops are densely clustered in busy urban centers. Examples include Meston Road (Kanpur), Dumdum (Kolkata), Karol Bagh (Delhi), and Zaveri Bazar (Mumbai).
    

### **6. Recent Crises & Precarity**

- **2016 Protests:** In March 2016, jewellers engaged in a 42-day strike across India to protest a 1% excise duty and a new rule mandating PAN details for cash purchases over ₹200,000.
    
- **Occupational Shift:** Continuous state regulations and shocks (like GST and demonetisation) have driven many artisans out of the trade into construction or small retail.
    

### **7. Key Sociological Concepts & Scholars Mentioned**

- **Mark Granovetter (1983):** Known for the "Strength of Weak Ties" theory. This study _challenges_ his theory, showing that for goldsmiths, **strong ties** (close village/family networks) are crucial for establishing the trust needed to enter the trade.
    
- **Jean Lave & Etienne Wenger (1991):** Their theory of apprenticeship learning within a "community of practice" is used to explain how young boys absorb goldsmithing skills organically through observation and immersion.
    
- **Barbara Harris-White (2005):** Argues that the informal economy is a "socially regulated economy" governed by caste, religion, and spatial clusters rather than state law.
    
- **James Coleman (1990):** His work on social capital is used to explain how artisans share and borrow expensive machinery to survive.
    
- **Rohit De (2010):** Cited regarding market governance and how state zeal to regulate often infringes on citizens' vital livelihood rights.
