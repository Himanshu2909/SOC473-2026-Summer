## Chapter 9: The Interdependence of Families and Jatis

### 1. Contractual and Jajmani Relations

- Civilization requires a complex specialization of work and regular exchange of products and services.
    
- Traditional specialization follows the preferred, permitted, and forbidden occupations of one's _jati_.
    
- Sir Henry Maine (1861) described these exchanges along a scale from "status" to "contract".
    
- Purely contractual relations are individual, impersonal, temporary, and limited.
    
- "Jajmani" relations are broadly supportive, group-oriented, long-term, and multiple.
    
- The term "jajmani" stems from the Hindi word studied extensively by William Wiser in 1936.
    
- Commercial transactions and contractual exchanges have historically existed alongside jajmani; for example, Totagadde village in Mysore has exported areca nuts for over six centuries.
    /
- Traditional occupations like weaving were often contractual, with the weaver paid for what they make and sell rather than receiving a customary harvest share.
    
- Jajmani links strictly exist between families, not between entire jatis.
    
- Jajmani relations are durable (often inherited across generations), exclusive (no poaching of clients), and multiple (involving social, ritual, and economic support).
    
- Village officials and servants (like watchmen) maintain jajmani ties with the entire village, collecting harvest contributions from every farmer.
    
- In Maharashtra, artisan families often hold rights to serve a specific geographic segment of a village rather than individual families.
    
- **Terminology:** The patron or recipient of services is the _jajman_. The provider is called _kamin_, _parjan_, _pardhan_, or in Marathi, _balutedar_.
    

### 2. Specialized Jatis and Multiple Functions

- Jajmani workers are divided into ritual specialists (e.g., priests, washermen, barbers) and artisans/unskilled laborers.
    
- Ritual specialists are essential for managing purity and pollution.
    
- Tasks involving excreta, hair cutting, dirty clothes, and childbirth are viewed as defiling.
    
- High-ranking ritual specialists (Brahmin priests) and lower-ranking ones (washermen, barbers) typically refuse to serve the lowest _Harijan_ families to avoid extreme degradation.
    
- Artisans generally deal with multiple patrons and are more independent than laborers, who are usually bound to a single landowning family.
    
- A major function of jajmani is mutual personal support during emergencies, ceremonies, or factional quarrels.
    
- In Lucknow district (studied by Majumdar), 10 of 14 jatis formally participate in a Thakur family marriage.
    
- Barbers play central managerial and ritual roles in weddings and births; their wives act as midwives and house-cleaners.
    
- Sikh villages even maintain jatis of Sikh barbers solely for their managerial and midwifery functions, despite prohibitions on hair cutting.
    
- In 1957 in Senapur, dominant Thakur landowners successfully boycotted Noniya earth-workers from building a school by leveraging regional jajmani network pressure.
    

### 3. Jajmani Payments and Obligations

- Jajmani rights are treated as inalienable property rights that can be divided among sons or sons-in-law.
    
- In Senapur (studied by Reddy), _Lohar_ blacksmiths fill the carpentry void and their work is divided by hereditary shares.
    
- Jati councils strictly punish encroachment on another family's jajmani clients.
    
- Work is calculated by a "yoke of cultivation" (land cultivated by one pair of bullocks), which equals about 15 _bighas_ or 10 acres.
    
- For one yoke, a Lohar works 60 to 70 man-hours annually and receives roughly 40 _seers_ (80 pounds) of grain.
    
- Traditional payments are dramatically handed over at the threshing floor during harvest time.
    
- Landowners provide additional perks: house sites, grazing rights, fuel, loans, and ceremonial gifts.
    
- Outside of jajmani obligations, specialists engage in wage-labor or piece-rate contracts (e.g., building a new house).
    

### 4. Supply, Demand, and Flexible Payments

- British administrative land records occasionally stipulated exact jajmani payments, but actual exchanges fluctuated with harvest bounty.
    
- In Rampura, Mysore (studied by Srinivas), specialists favor patrons who pay annually in grain over those paying in cash.
    
- Shortages in skills lead to importing specialists or allowing families to take up sub-occupations, while surpluses force workers into agriculture or migration.
    
- In Poona district (studied by Orenstein), untouchable menials, carpenters, and blacksmiths receive the largest total amounts, while Brahmin priests receive smaller amounts per patron but serve more families.
    
- In Sherurpur (studied by Gould), compensation strictly aligns with traditional rank: washermen receive the lowest pay, barbers next, and carpenters/blacksmiths the highest.
    
- Jajmani networks establish an unbroken web of relationships connecting multiple villages across linguistic regions.
    

### 5. Enforcement: Coercion vs. Consensus

- Jati councils enforce minimum standards of exchange and settle disputes.
    
- Conflict often arises when dominant landowners use collective power (withholding pay, physical harassment) against artisan groups, who may retaliate with service boycotts.
    
- Scholars like Beidelman, Lewis, and Barnouw view jajmani strictly as a coercive system used by the rich to exploit the poor.
    
- Scholars like Rao, Orenstein, Berreman, Kolenda, and Gould argue the system also operates on consensus, providing social solidarity and mutual benefits.
    

### 6. Change and Continuity in Modern Jajmani

- Jajmani is declining due to monetization, improved transport, and the rise of cash crops (which are excluded from traditional barter).
    
- Political changes and reduced local isolation have weakened the coercive power of dominant landowners.
    
- Despite declines, many villagers prefer jajmani for its credit facilities, employment security, and reliable ritual services.
    
- A Maharashtra survey (Karve and Damle) showed over two-thirds of respondents favored jajmani arrangements primarily for credit facilities.
    
- In Totagadde (studied by Harper), despite a highly commercialized areca nut economy, concepts of obligation and favors continue to stabilize economic relationships.
    
- In Barmer district, Rajasthan (studied by Bose and Jodha), 75% of households still maintain ties with leatherworkers, primarily for ritual/death pollution tasks.
    
- In Sherurpur, U.P. (studied by Gould), sugarcane bypasses the jajmani system; only 1.5% of the total grain harvest is distributed as fixed payments.
    
- Thakurs and Brahmins in Sherurpur maintain the highest proportion of traditional ties (70%) to ensure their social importance and ritual purity.
    

## Chapter 10: Criteria for the Ranking of Jatis

### 1. Manifestations of Rank in Daily Life

- The social order and hierarchy dictate strict limits on interpersonal dealings.
    
- Jati rank is continuously manifested in commonplace, daily details.
    
- Seating arrangements at gossip sessions or council meetings strictly follow spatial hierarchy.
    
- In Kanpur District, a cot is used as a bench to symbolize authority.
    
- Brahmins occupy the highest status position at the head of the cot.
    
- Thakurs sit next to the Brahmins and precede all lower ranks.
    
- Lower-ranking individuals sit at the foot of the cot.
    
- The lowest jatis must sit entirely on the ground.
    
- Within the ground-sitters, internal hierarchy is still maintained; a _Chamar_ (leatherworker) will only allow a _Dhanuk_ (pigkeeper) to sit at the lowest part of a shared space.
    
- Spatial positioning directly correlates to expected authority, where those at the head of the cot are expected to lead conversations and establish consensus.
