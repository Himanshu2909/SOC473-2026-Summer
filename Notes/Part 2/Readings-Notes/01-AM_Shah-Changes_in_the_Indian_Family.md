- **Westernisation:** The process affecting higher castes that tends to _diminish_ the size and number of relationships within a household.

- **Henry Maine:** The scholar who laid the foundation for the sociological study of the Indian family by comparing the Indian joint family with the Western individual family, relying heavily on Indological texts.

- **Shraddha:** A ritual mentioned in the Shastras; the circle of persons required to perform this ritual closely coincides with the legal definition of the joint property group.

## 1. Sanskritisation vs Westernisation — opposite effects (→ A3)

This is a **direction-of-effect** pair. Shah sets these up as opposites, not parallels:

- **Sanskritisation** (of lower castes / Adivasi tribes) → **increases** household size and number of relationships. _Why:_ Sanskritisation means lower castes start emulating the customs/ideals of higher, more "Sanskritised" castes. Since higher castes are more influenced by scriptures that idealise a large, multi-generational household living together (the "residential unity of patri-kin" norm), adopting Sanskritised norms means adopting the norm of staying together longer/more completely.
    
- **Westernisation** (of higher/urban castes) → **diminishes** household size and number of relationships. _Why:_ Westernisation pulls in the opposite direction — toward individualism/nuclear living, away from the scriptural ideal of residential unity.
    

**Memory hook:** Sanskritisation = "moving toward the old orthodox ideal" = bigger household. Westernisation = "moving toward the modern individual ideal" = smaller household. They are mirror-image forces acting on different populations.

_So for A3: Statement 1 (Sanskritisation increases) is TRUE. Statement 2 (Westernisation increases) is FALSE — Westernisation decreases, not increases._

## 2. Simple vs Complex/Joint Household — precise definitions (→ A4)

Don't oversimplify these definitions — the exact wording matters:

|Type|Exact composition|
|---|---|
|**Simple household**|A complete elementary family **OR a part of** an elementary family (e.g., a widow alone, a single parent + child — these are still "simple" even though not a _complete_ elementary family)|
|**Complex/joint household**|Two or more elementary families, OR parts of two or more elementary families, OR one elementary family + parts of one or more other elementary families|

**Key trap:** "Simple household" is NOT restricted to _only_ a complete elementary family — it also includes partial elementary families (e.g., a widowed mother and her unmarried son is still "simple," not "complex," because it's only fragments of _one_ family line, not multiple families combining).

_So for A4: Statement 1 ("only composed of a complete elementary family") is TRUE—FALSE, actually — it's too narrow, so it's FALSE as stated (since "only" excludes the "part of an elementary family" case). Statement 2 (complex household may include parts of two or more elementary families) is TRUE._

## 3. Developmental Process: Progression, Regression, and the Fortes Disagreement (→ A7)

- **Progression** = household grows (via birth, marriage — i.e., new members added)
- **Regression** = household shrinks (via marriage [daughters marrying out], **death**, and **partition**)

Shah explicitly states there is _a pattern_ to this developmental process, **but he explicitly rejects Fortes's claim that it is cyclical**. This is a specific citation moment (footnote to Jack Goody's edited volume, _The Developmental Cycle in Domestic Groups_, 1958) where Shah distances himself from an established anthropological theory.

**Key trap:** Don't assume Shah agrees with every scholar he cites — citing a work is often to _disagree_ with it. Fortes = cyclical view; Shah = pattern exists, but NOT cyclical.

_So for A7: Statement 1 (regression via marriage/death/partition) is TRUE. Statement 2 (Shah agrees with Fortes on cyclicality) is FALSE._

## 4. The Gujarat Statistics — memorize this table (→ A9, A10, A11)

Shah gives **two separate villages** and **one detailed statistical breakdown**. Keep these straight:

**(a) Village-to-village comparison (qualitative):**

- Village #1 (Shah's main studied village): **NO case** of two or more married brothers living together _after their parents' death_.
- **Nearby village**: has a **considerable number** of such households.
- **Point of this comparison:** it shows the _maximum extent_ to which the developmental process/norm is carried out **varies between villages** — even ones close to each other.

**(b) Statistical breakdown (in the main studied village):**

|Household type|% of total households|
|---|---|
|One/both parents + **2 or more** married sons + wives/children|**5%**|
|One/both parents + **1** married son|**19%**|

**(c) Two separate "case" breakdowns (not percentages of total households — percentages _within_ each case type):**

|Case type|Total cases|Living jointly|Living separately|
|---|---|---|---|
|Parents with **2+ married sons**|41 cases|12 (29.26%)|29 (70.73%)|
|Parents with **1 married son**|50 cases|38 (**76%**)|12 (**24%**)|

**Key trap:** These four numbers (5%, 19%, 29.26%/70.73%, 76%/24%) come from **two different statistical frames** — don't confuse "% of all households" (5%, 19%) with "% within a specific case-count" (29.26%, 76%). Also notice the _logic_: sons with only **one** married son are **much more likely** to stay joint (76%) than families with **multiple** married sons (only 29.26% stay joint) — Shah's point is that as more sons marry, the household is _more_ likely to split, not less.

_So for A9: both statements are TRUE (this is the "both true" case). For A10: both TRUE (5% and 19% are correct). For A11: Statement 1 says 29.26% is for "all sons living with parents" — that's correct for the 2+ sons case. Statement 2 says 76% is for one married son — also correct. So A11: both TRUE._

## 6. Mitakshara vs Dayabhaga — the critical legal contrast (→ A14, A15)

This is the single most detail-heavy legal point in the article. Two competing schools of Hindu law:

||Mitakshara|Dayabhaga|
|---|---|---|
|Basis of coparcenary|**Birth** — a son acquires an interest in property _by being born_|No birth-right coparcenary|
|Father + unmarried son|**Sufficient** to constitute a coparcenary (because the son already has a birth-interest)|**Never** constitutes a coparcenary between father and son(s), **married or unmarried**, **even if living in a single household**|

**Key trap (A14):** Statement 2 asks about Dayabhaga — and reverses the rule. Dayabhaga explicitly says there is **NO** coparcenary between father and son regardless of marital status or shared residence. So "automatically constitute a coparcenary" under Dayabhaga is **FALSE** — it's the opposite of what Dayabhaga holds.

**On coparceners vs maintenance-right holders (A15):**

- **Coparceners** = males only (self, sons, son's sons, son's grandsons under Mitakshara)
- **Wives and unmarried daughters** = NOT coparceners — they only have a **right to maintenance**, not ownership/inheritance rights as coparceners.

_So for A14: Statement 1 TRUE (Mitakshara), Statement 2 FALSE (reverses Dayabhaga). For A15: Statement 1 FALSE (wives/daughters are NOT coparceners), Statement 2 TRUE (maintenance right only)._

## 7. Shraddha Ritual and the Joint Family Definition (→ A18)

Two separate ideas here that the question deliberately entangles:

1. **Does the Shraddha-performing circle need to live together?** — **NO.** Shah explicitly parallels this to the joint property group: just as a joint property group need not live in one household, neither does the circle of persons obligated to perform the Shraddha for a person.
2. **Does the legal definition of joint family coincide with who performs Shraddha?** — **YES.** Shah argues this coincidence (property rights ≈ ritual obligation) is _precisely why_ the Indological/Shastric definition became accepted as "the" definition of the Hindu joint family.

**Key trap:** The word "must" in statement 1 reverses the actual claim — the whole point is that residence is _not_ required.

_So for A18: Statement 1 FALSE, Statement 2 TRUE._

## 8. Henry Maine's Evolutionary Theory (→ A19) — get the direction right

Maine compared:

- **Indian joint family** (traditional, collective)
- **Western individual/elementary family** (modern, individual)

His conclusion: the **Western individual family is LATER in evolution** than the Indian joint family (i.e., joint family = more "primitive"/earlier stage; individual family = more "advanced"/later stage in a unilinear evolutionary scheme).

**Key trap:** It's easy to flip this and think Maine ranked the Indian joint family as "later" (more advanced) — he did NOT. He ranked it as **earlier**, and the Western nuclear family as the more evolved, later stage. This is exactly what A19 tests — statement 1 says "Indian joint family as a LATER stage… than the Western family" — that's backwards from what Maine argued.

_So for A19: Statement 1 is FALSE (reversed). Statement 2 (based on Indological-cum-legal material) is TRUE._

## 9. What the Indological Literature Does and Doesn't Provide (→ A20)

The Indological/Shastric texts:

- **DO NOT** provide: any data on the various _types_ of households, or the _frequency_ of each type, across different sections of society. There was never a "census" in ancient India.
- **DO** provide: an _ideal_ — a household of **four generations** — and this ideal was mainly emulated by **higher castes** (who were more influenced by the scriptures).
- The texts are also skewed: mostly about **Brahmins and a few other higher castes**, not society broadly.

**Key trap:** "Good comparative data on household types/frequencies across castes" is precisely what Shah says the Indological literature _lacks_ — it's an ideal/normative text, not an empirical/statistical one.

_So for A20: Statement 1 FALSE, Statement 2 TRUE._

## 10. Shah's Own Gujarat Census Analysis (1820-30) (→ A21)

Two separate findings from Shah's analysis of the **early 19th-century** Gujarat census data: (a) **Average household size = 4.5** (b) Progressive development of households **rarely, if ever, went BEYOND** the phase of co-residence of two or more married sons during their parents' lifetime.

**Key trap:** Statement 2 says progression "frequently went beyond" this stage — that's the reverse of Shah's actual finding. His point is that even in the early 19th century, households **rarely progressed further** than that stage (i.e., very large, multi-generation joint households extending further were rare) — reinforcing his thesis that huge joint households were not the historical norm.

_So for A21: Statement 1 TRUE, Statement 2 FALSE._

## 11. Ghurye's Maharashtra Data — the comparison point (→ A22)

Shah cites Professor Ghurye's book _After a Century and a Quarter_ (Popular Book Depot, Bombay, 1960) as an **independent confirmation** from a different region (Maharashtra, not Gujarat) using data from the **same period** (1820s).

**The finding:** the Maharashtra village shows **the SAME average household size** as the Gujarat village (i.e., also around 4.5) — NOT a larger one.

**Why this matters for Shah's argument:** this cross-regional agreement strengthens his claim that small household size was a general, not a Gujarat-specific, historical pattern.

_So for A22: Statement 1 FALSE (same, not larger), Statement 2 TRUE (book title is correct)._

## 12. E A Gait's 1911 Census Comparison (→ A24)

Gait's actual point was the **opposite** of "far higher than Europe" — he said India's figure (4.9) was **"much the same as in European countries."** He then gave the comparison figures: Scotland 4.8, England & Wales 5.2 — i.e., India's household size was **comparable to, not dramatically different from, Europe's.**

**Why this matters:** this is one of Shah's key pieces of evidence against the "India = huge traditional households" myth — even a British colonial census official, working within the very system that assumed India had abnormally large households, found figures **in line with Europe**.

_So for A24: Statement 1 FALSE ("far higher" is wrong — Gait said "much the same"). Statement 2 TRUE (correct comparison figures)._

## 13. Orenstein's Findings — don't reverse the trend (→ A26, A27)

Orenstein's actual finding (1867 to 1951 census data): average household size **did NOT decrease** — in fact, there was a **slight tendency to INCREASE** (Shah emphasizes: "very slight indeed").

**Conclusion Orenstein draws (and Shah agrees with):** the belief that large/joint households were widely prevalent in **pre-British India** is **probably false** — because if it were true, and modernisation broke down joint households, we'd expect to see household size _decrease_ over the census period. Since it didn't decrease (even inched up slightly), the "big traditional household" premise doesn't hold up.

_So for A26: Statement 1 is FALSE (reverses the actual trend — it did NOT decrease). Statement 2 is TRUE._

**On the demographic explanation (A27):**

- Orenstein's suggestion: the slight increase might be due to a rise in the average number of **children** or **adults** per household — i.e., a demographic/biological explanation rather than a "families are becoming more joint" explanation.
- Shah's own gloss on this (in parentheses in the text): **IF** the increase is due to more **adults** (not children) per household, **THEN** this would actually **strengthen** the argument that there's an increasing overall emphasis on large/joint households — because more co-resident adults (as opposed to just more surviving children) suggests more married couples/generations staying together.

**Key trap:** This is a conditional ("if adults, then strengthens joint-family argument") — don't misread it as Shah stating this as settled fact; it's a conditional inference he draws from Orenstein's conjecture.

_So for A27: Statement 1 TRUE, Statement 2 TRUE (both true)._
