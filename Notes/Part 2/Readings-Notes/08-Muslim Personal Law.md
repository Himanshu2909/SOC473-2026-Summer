## 1. Model Nikahnama & Muslim Women's Organisations (H1)

**Who made gender-just model nikahnamas?**

- **Bharatiya Muslim Mahila Andolan (BMMA)**
- **All India Muslim Women Personal Law Board (AIMWPLB)**

Both of these are groups _fighting for_ Muslim women's rights, and both created their own versions of a "gender-just" model marriage contract (nikahnama).

**Contrast with:** the **All India Muslim Personal Law Board (AIMPLB)** — this is the body that stands for/defends traditional Muslim personal law (not the one that made the gender-just nikahnamas). Note how close the names are — this is the classic exam trap:

- AIMWPLB = Women's board → gender-just nikahnama
- AIMPLB = the general/traditional board → defends existing personal law

**Why aren't model nikahnamas actually used?** A community leader told the authors: ordinary people go to **local imams** to get married, and these imams are themselves poor/struggling and don't even know the model nikahnama exists. So a well-drafted document on paper doesn't survive contact with ground realities of poverty and unemployment ("codified texts... wilt in these labyrinths of collective unemployment, underemployment, and poverty").

## 2. Case Studies at the Darul Qaza (F1, F2, F5, F6, F8)

Keep these four/five case vignettes straight — they're easy to mix up:

|Case|What happened|Where first resolved / who intervened|Outcome|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Sister-in-law "controlling" husband**|Wife accused husband's sister of controlling him; husband wasn't paying maintenance|Case started at a **mahila thana**, then brought to darul qaza|Ended in **divorce**|
|**Elopement case**|Husband eloped with his wife's younger sister but wouldn't divorce the elder (first) wife|Qazi couldn't resolve it → wife's family convened a **mohalla panchayat**|Elder sister released from marriage; younger sister declared the wife (a _samjhauta_/settlement)|
|**Abduction case**|Two sisters rushed in, sobbing — their **brother was abducted by his brothers-in-law**|—|Qazi **consoled them and told them to report it to the local police station** (i.e., he didn't investigate himself — he gave counsel and redirected them to the appropriate forum)|
|**Stepmother case**|Stepmother complained she was **thrown out of the house by her stepdaughter**|—|(illustrates disputes rooted in kinship/household, not just husband-wife)|
|**Mother + children vs. husband**|Long-drawn case: mother and her children teamed up against the husband, accusing him of an **extramarital affair**|—|(another kinship-rooted dispute)|

**Key general point (F8 and related):** The article stresses that **a large number of vicious fights brought to the darul qaza were between sisters-in-law**, fighting over family resources and emotional/relational stakes ("affect"). This supports the broader argument that conjugal strain is often really rooted in the **wider matrix of kinship**, not simply between husband and wife.

So for F8: **both statements are true** — (1) the mother-and-children-vs-husband case is real, and (2) sisters-in-law conflicts were indeed common. → Answer: **a) Both true**

## 3. Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act, 1986 [MWA] (C5, C6, C8)

- **Passed: 1986**
- **Passed in the aftermath of the Shah Bano judgment** (the Supreme Court's Shah Bano ruling on maintenance for a divorced Muslim woman caused a political backlash, leading Parliament to pass the MWA).
- **Widely seen as anti-women** at the time (perceived as a rollback of the Shah Bano gains, since it seemed to remove Muslim women from the ambit of Section 125 CrPC maintenance).
- **BUT in practice**, research (Agnes 2001; Solanki 2011) shows judges used the MWA creatively to give divorced Muslim women "**reasonable and fair**" compensation — sometimes **more** than what they'd get under other personal laws or under Section 125 CrPC.
- **Limitation (Vatuk 2017):** Works fine for families with money; but for **poor** women it's very hard to get the order **executed**, because an unemployed/low-income husband simply doesn't have the capacity to pay.

**So for C6:** Statement 1 (MWA seen as anti-women) = **true**. Statement 2 (judges used it to give fair/generous compensation) = **true**. → **a) Both true**

**Section 498A CrPC (C8):** This is the **dowry harassment / domestic violence** criminal provision. Women sometimes filed 498A complaints **at the same time as** seeking khula at the darul qaza — the criminal pressure of 498A pushed a reluctant husband to actually show up and negotiate at the darul qaza. (Don't confuse with **Section 125 CrPC**, which is about **maintenance**, not harassment.)

## 4. Core Argument / Authors' Stance (B4, B5, B6, B7)

**B4 — Agency, not passivity:** The authors explicitly **reject** the idea that Muslim women are just passive "objects of reform." Their whole ethnographic point is the opposite: women actively **use multiple forums** (civil courts, darul qaza, mahila thana, mohalla committees) and **strategically move between them**, even using rulings from one forum to strengthen their case in another. This shows **agency**. → Statement 1 (women = passive objects) is **false**; Statement 2 (ethnography shows agency across forums, even "patriarchal" ones) is **true**. → Answer: **c) Statement 2 true, Statement 1 false**

**B5 — Stance on the triple talaq judgment:** Not a blanket celebration, not a rejection — it's a **qualified welcome**: "welcomed... as an important step," a "landmark legal victory," **but** criticized by others (and implicitly by the authors) as **ambiguous** and leaving too much room for personal law.

**B6 — Who called it "ambiguous"?** **Pratap Bhanu Mehta** (cited: Mehta 2017, "Small Step, No Giant Leap," Indian Express) — he's the one quoted as saying the verdict was ambiguous and didn't do enough for constitutional rights.

- Don't confuse with **Flavia Agnes**, who is quoted separately about the _hearing_ (legal luminaries not knowing ground realities), not about the verdict being "ambiguous."

**B7 — Why be cautious about the policing system handling personal law:** Two linked reasons:

1. Criminal law gives the **police unbridled discretionary power**.
2. There is already documented **bias by class, caste, gender, and religion** in how law gets implemented on the ground. → So pushing personal law disputes further into the criminal/policing system may not actually deliver "real gains" for the women it's meant to help — it could just funnel them into a system with its own biases.

## 5. Scholars & Their Specific Contributions (E1–E13)

This is the section with the most "who said what" traps. Memorize this table:

|Scholar(s)|What they studied / found|
|---|---|
|**Gopika Solanki**|Fine-grained analysis: legal practice in family law is **localised & decentralised** across many actors — lawyers, clergy, family, religious orgs, sect councils, women's orgs, "doorstep courts" (residential committees, women's ad hoc groups). Book: _Adjudication in Religious Family Laws_|
|**A Suneetha & Vasudha Nagaraj**|Hyderabad research: women's **trajectory** before reaching a police station — natal family → community leaders → caste sanghams (councils) → basti-level women's groups → influential local personalities → _then_ family counselling centre/police|
|**Patricia Jeffery**|Rural Uttar Pradesh (**Bijnor**), 2001: found **little difference** in hardship between Hindu and Muslim women after marital breakdown — kinship/custom/poverty mattered more than religion/legal status|
|**Srimati Basu**|Family courts & **police-attached mediation units**: showed civil society actors (influential citizens, social workers, NGO staff, professionals) — who have **no formal enforcement power** — could still defuse conflict and get couples to comply with settlements|
|**A Suneetha** (solo, 2012)|Muslim women activists using **"haq" (right)** and **"taleem" (education)** to build self-reliance/moral responsibility in education, family dispute resolution, and economic survival — breaks down the citizenship-vs-community binary|
|**Flavia Agnes**|(1) Noted that during the 6-day triple talaq hearing, even top legal experts hadn't researched ground realities. (2) Also wrote on MWA outcomes (Agnes 2001)|
|**Faisal Devji**|Argued personal law could be like Hajj — non-controversial if **state-regulated, non-sectarian, efficient, de-politicised**. Authors push back: "administering" personal law is unclear because of sectarian splits (Barelvi vs Deobandi) in ritual and caste-like _biradari_ divisions in kinship|
|**Faizan Mustafa**|Pointed to the **impossibility of judging marriage/its failure by the yardstick of constitutionality**|
|**Katherine Lemons**|PhD dissertation: _At the Margins of Law: Adjudicating Muslim Families in Contemporary Delhi_|
|**Sylvia Vatuk**|Multiple works; 2017 book: _Marriage and Its Discontents: Women, Islam and the Law in India_ — includes the finding on MWA being hard to enforce for poor women|

**Memory hooks:**

- **Solanki = decentralised/multiple actors** (think "S for Spread-out system")
- **Suneetha+Nagaraj = trajectory/order of forums** (Hyderabad)
- **Jeffery = Hindu/Muslim similarity in rural UP (Bijnor)**
- **Basu = mediation units, no formal power but still effective**
- **Suneetha solo = haq + taleem** (activist concepts)
- **Devji = Hajj analogy** ↔ **authors' rebuttal = sectarian/biradari splits**
- **Mustafa = "can't judge marriage by constitutionality"**
- **Lemons = Delhi PhD thesis**
- **Vatuk = 2017 book title + poor women/MWA enforcement problem**

**E13:** Jeffery's fieldwork = **rural UP (Bijnor)** = true. Suneetha & Nagaraj's fieldwork = **Hyderabad**, NOT Delhi = false. → Answer: **b) Statement 1 true, Statement 2 false**
