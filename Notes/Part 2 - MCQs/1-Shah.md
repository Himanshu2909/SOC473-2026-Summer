### Quick-reference fact sheet (for last-minute revision)

- **Author / Source:** A M Shah, _Economic and Political Weekly_, Annual Number, January 1968, pp. 127–134.
- **Average household size (early 19th c., Gujarat & Maharashtra villages):** 4.5
- **1911 Census (Gait):** 4.9 per house — comparable to Scotland (4.8), England & Wales (5.2)
- **Henry Orenstein:** _"The Recent History of the Extended Family in India"_, _Social Problems_, Vol 8, No 4, Spring 1961 — showed household size didn't decrease 1867–1951; slight increase.
- **Henry Maine:** used Indological-cum-legal material; ranked Western individual family as a _later_ evolutionary stage than Indian joint family.
- **Mitakshara vs Dayabhaga:** Mitakshara — coparcenary by birth (self + sons + son's sons + son's grandsons); Dayabhaga — no coparcenary between father and sons regardless of residence.
- **Legal joint family** = coparcenary males + their wives + unmarried daughters (latter: maintenance right only, not coparceners).
- **Fortes/Goody** (_Developmental Cycle in Domestic Groups_, 1958) — cyclical view of household development; **Shah rejects this** as not cyclical.
- **Gujarat statistics:** 41 cases (2+ married sons): 12 (29.26%) joint, 29 (70.73%) separate. 50 cases (1 married son): 38 (76%) joint, 12 (24%) separate.
- **Key reversal claim:** towns (not villages) historically showed greater residential unity/joint-household emphasis, because towns had proportionally more Sanskritised castes.
- **Captain Cruikshank:** conducted the original Gujarat census schedules (1820–30) later compiled into Bhattacharya & Bhattacharya's _Census of India 1961_ population estimates volume.
- **Ghurye:** _After a Century and a Quarter_ (1960) — Maharashtra village data matching Gujarat's 4.5 average.
- **Srinivas:** Sanskritisation/Westernisation framework — Shah recommends applying this instead of assuming one uniform trajectory of change.
