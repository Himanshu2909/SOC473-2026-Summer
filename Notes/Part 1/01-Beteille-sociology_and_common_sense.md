## I

1. Common sense is particular and localized; Sociological knowledge aims to be general (if not universal). Common sense is variable; and subject to constraints of time, place, and other social factors.

2. Issues faced by sociology:
    - bias can be induced via the sociologist's localized common sense (this bias has to be removed via certain methods of critique)
    - over complication of analysis, leading to disconnection from the question at hand

3. Technicalities, and common sense both are important. Hence none can be abandoned during sociological analysis. But there should be a balance of both in order to ensure that neither does the bias of localized common sense does take over, nor do unnecessary technical jargons lead to a drift from the question at hand.

4. Issues faced in teaching sociology:
    - Not a factual subject: arguments and counterarguments for the same claim can exist unlike scientific or historical facts where a particulat statement can be judged as either right or wrong
    - Greater ambiguity and disagreement
    - It requires combination of a substantial arugment and good writing (ie, well presented substantive arguments)

5. A good student of sociology is one who has knowledge and understanding of different kinds of societies. They can only construct good arguments when they are pushed out of their locality (knowledge about their own society), and forced to analyze other, diverse societal structures as well.
    - Hence, the general equipment for sociological analysis, accumulated/aggregted concepts, general methods and data of sociology becomes more important than localized knowledge of society.
    - This is what differentiates common sense with systematic sociological study
    - Analysis can be more rigorous when localized structures are analyzed through the lens of these accumulated concepts; and this will also give rise to better findings

6. Issue: Common sense dressed with jargon looks like sociology

## II

- Sociological Works are heavily dominated by western ideas, and this makes any accumulated concept biased towards the common sense and structures of the western society.
- Solely analyzing eastern societies via these accumulated lenses which have a heavy western bias leads to bad analysis 
- Hence, it is possible for accumulated concepts of sociology to be mistaken, distorted and obsolete
- Therefore, one must approach any concept with critique rather than just trying to apply it to a localized society directly
(ex nihilo: out of nothing)
- A good way to come up with a new sociological concept would not be to only analyze the eastern perspective; but to be able to accumulate the ideas of both, eastern and western societies and make a general theory.
- Claim:  observations of Emile Durkheim and Max Weber on other societies were limited, one-sided and often mislead because they were not well accustomed to them
- ED and MW believed that systematic comparisons between their societies and other societies would deepen sociological understanding
- For these comparisons, specialized tools of enquiry and analysis are necessary.
- Arguments of **Emile Durkheim** against use of common sense:
    - Sociological concepts and questions are proximate to one's day to day lives
    - People have limited social experience because they live most of their lives in one region or limited number of regions. These specific regions have their specialized cultures, ideas, narratives and societal structures
    - People have preconceptions about sociological ideas because of this limited environment/limited social experience. This is the so called 'common sense'.
    - These preconceptions are not only more than often wrong, but also act as impediments to the examination of available facts.
    - In order for systematic and generalized investigation of a question, one must not rely on common sense and localized understanding.
- Durkheim: Study of Suicide:
    - distinction between the rate of suicide and incidence of suicide
    - suicide rates varied systematically between societies, and various different religions, occupational and other groups of the same society.
    - suicide rates fluctuated because of social and economic causes
    - suicide rates go up not only after an economic crash but also after an economic boom
        - Higher suicides during economic crisis due to:
            - Financial loss and debt
            - Loss of identity and self worth
            - Strained relationships: breakups, domestic conflicts, social withdrawl, removal of critial support systems
        - Higher suicides during economic booms due to:
            - overwhelming pressure to perform and succeed
            - Disillusionment due to rising expectations: boom might only benefit a certain section of the population, and for others it's just an unfulfillment of expectations and jealosy
            - Social Disruption and social isolation: modernization and shifting societal norms can erode community structures, leaving individuals feeling alienated and disconnected.
            - [Durkheim] Anomie: State of normlessness, where rapid economic expansion strips away established rules and creates a chaotic environment where individuals lack a clear sense of boundaries or purpose.
            - Modern Study: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S246826672400152X
            - These reasonings might not be true in all cases of economic boom, and in the modern day (the arguments are conditional on scale and time)
    - If something goes against common sense - rather than rejecting it - as a sociologist one must collect data and systematically analyze it
- Max Weber on religion and economics:
    - Consequences of action and actor intention might not align most of the times (sometimes, diametrically opposite)
    - Worked on correlating religious values and economic action
    - Before weber: for good or evil, religion had served as a source of social stability
        - Marx: change is necessary and desirable -> religion has a negative value
        - Durkheim: stability was essential for social well-being -> religion has a positive value
    - Weber: tension between the commitment to ideal values (preached by religion) and demands of material existence - is the true source of change in society

## III

- Fundamental preoccupations of sociology:
    1. interconnections among the different domains of society
    2. comparisons
- 'functionalist bias' of sociology
    - Structural functionalism: society is a complex system whose parts work together to promote solidarity and stability
    - Assumes that social institutions and cultural norms exist to serve a positive, unifying purpose
    - The assumption that structural functionalism is true leads to multiple issues:
        1. Conservative Ideology: By focusing heavily on how parts of society work together to maintain equilibrium, functionalism implicitly supports the status quo and views change as inherently destabilizing.
        2. Justification of Inequality: Functionalists like Talcott Parsons often argue that social stratification (e.g., class and power differences) exists because it ensures the most important roles are filled by the most qualified people, thereby normalizing inequality.
        3. Assumption of Consensus: The theory operates on the assumption that society is built on a "value consensus" (shared norms and values), ignoring systemic power imbalances, oppression, and coercion.
        4. Teleology: Functionalism is often accused of logical fallacies, such as assuming that because an institution exists, it must be the result of some inherent need, rather than historical accident or the deliberate actions of dominant groups.
- Finding interconnections between institutions is not bad, but assuming that these interconnections exist to establish harmony and stability in the society leads to the multiple flaws highlighted above
- 'social integration' v/s 'system integration' (David Lockwood, 'Social Integration and System Integration' in his Solidarity and Schism)
- M N Srinivas: 'book-view' v/s 'field-view' (M N Srinivas, India's Villages; M N Srinivas, Caste in Modern India and Other Essays): via example of varna v/s jati
- A M Shah: the average size of the household in the Indian population has remained roughly the same in the last hundred years.
- Observation of author: Indian authors do not use the method of comparisons, and restrict themselves to social structures in their own region
- On the method of comparisons:
    - Durkheim:  comparisons can include facts borrowed from:
        - a single and unique society
        - several societies of the same species, or 
        - several distinct social species
    - Example: Positive Discrimination in India v/s Affirmative Action in US:
        - Andre Beteille, 'Equality as a Right and as a Policy', LSE Quarterly, Vol 1, No 1, 1987, pp 75-98; 
        - Andre Beteille, 'Distributive Justice and Institutional Well-Being', Economic and Political Weekly, Vol XXVI, Nos 11 and 12, 199 1, pp 591-600.

- Common sense:
    - unreflective as it does not question it's own presuppositions
    - based on a limited range of experience of particular persons in particular places and time
- Comparative Sociology
- Sociological reasoning allows:
    - moderation of utopia (what is grounded in reality)
    - moderation of fatalism (events are predetermined by fate or destiny), ie acknowledging the existence of realistic change in social order
    - ie, sociology is anti-utopian and anti-fatalistic
- Note: ever social arrangement comes with a cost and it is tough to weigh out the costs and benefits of different arrangements because of the inherent subjectivity
- Value judgements v/s realistic judgements (principle v/s utility):
    -  Emile Durkheim, 'Value Judgments and Judgments of Reality' in his Sociology and Philosophy, The Free Press, Glencoe; 1974, pp 80-97; 
    - Max Weber, The Methodology of the Social Sciences, The Free Press, Glencoe, 1949
- Following the scientific sociological method minimizes bias and localized common sense
- Can values be treated as facts? If yes then how do you make a distinction between value judgements and realistic judgements? Does this make sociology utopian?
- Same subject matter can reveal different aspects/results when studied form different standpoints/perspectives, but these differences need not be contradictory. These differences are also important because they allow mutual correction (argument -> rebuttal/counter-argument -> repeat)
